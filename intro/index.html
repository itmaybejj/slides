...WCAG?

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines turn subjective tips:
("pick an easy to see color")

Into objective criteria:
“...[Small] text and images of text have a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1...”

A? AA? AAA?

"AA" is the federal (and Princeton) standard.

"AAA" is...aspirational. Don't worry about AAA.

Principal #3:
Differentiate with more than color alone ("in green") or a single sensory characteristic ("at left").

Color alone...

8% of men and ~0.5% of women have some form of colorblindness.

Pretty colors are great, but differentiate and test in black and white!

Secondary differentiation:

From:
"Course materials are in the green cart."

To:

Curb cut 5:
Structure content with headings, lists and table headers.

Headings?

For structure, not visual weight!
  • H1: Headings Create the Page Outline
    • H2: They outline major topics
      • H3: And subtopics
      • H3: And related subtopics
        • H4: And something nested with more depth
        • H4: And something else
    • H2: And then introduce another major topic
      • H3: It might have subtopics too

Screen reader users rely on heading levels

(Real) lists help too

Lots of authors think people want to read on the Web. They like to write nice long paragraphs with lots of filler words, and the key points hidden in amongst all those words. Few people read all the way to here, much less so anyone with ADHD, dyslexia, etc. In fact, we once had a major crises because a student missed a critical task. Turns out the critical link was buried in a paragraph like this, and titled with the dreaded here.

Structure that content!
  1. Lists are navigable
  2. Lists are easy to skim
    • The format encourages brevity
  3. They reflow on phones
    • Remember to use real list formatting
      • Don't fake it with * • - etc.

Table formatting

Structure that table!
  1. Assign headers
  2. Add caption if possible

What about images?

Curb cut #6:
Write alternative text that describes what each image means in this particular context.

What makes a good alt?

Write for brevity & contextual meaning.

  • Probably not: "Image of a small brown dog standing on a hardwood floor, holding a green tennis ball, pressed against her owner's knee. A dining room table is in the background"
  • Probably not: "a dog"
  • Perhaps: "Hazel pressing her ball against my knee, asking to play."
  • Or maybe: "Hazel before being groomed; she is super fluffy and has fur over her face."

Context changes how we describe things.

Pick a context, suggest an alt:

  • Article about the dog breed.
  • Article about tennis balls.
  • Article about a library study break.
  • Article about the dog's owner.

Who does this help?

It only helps 4 of these...

  1. People who are blind
  2. People who are deafblind
  3. People with low vision
  4. People who are colorblind
  5. People reading emails without allowing downloads
  6. People searching on Google

If you remember nothing else...

Accessibility is the art of making good content good for everybody. It can't take overly wordy, confusingly worded, poorly organized content and make it good.

Be brief, be clear, and you're more than halfway there.
